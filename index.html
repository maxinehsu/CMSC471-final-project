INTRO: Why Does This Matter?

What is disease?

Disease is any harm that is caused to an individual—in this case, humans. Infectious diseases are diseases that can be transmitted from one person to another, whether that is through coughing, sneezing, physical contact, consumption of contaminated food or water, contact with bodily fluids, or a vector (intermediary) like mosquitoes and ticks. Today, we are looking specifically at infectious diseases caused by viruses (and bacteria). Some of the worst viral (and bacterial) diseases are measles, diphtheria, pertussis (also called whooping cough), and polio, all of which we will be looking at data for below.

What are vaccines?

Vaccines are a treatment that gives a weakened form of the virus to a person's immune system, which teaches their immune system how to fight off the virus in case they get the normal (strong) version of the virus in the future. Vaccines are basically the "practice battle" to prepare an army against the "real battle" that may or may not happen. Vaccines can be highly effective against viruses; in fact, the measles vaccine is estimated to have saved 93.71 million lives since it was first introduced1.

Why is measles so bad?

Measles is the most infectious human disease on Earth. This is because, for each person that is infected with measles, an average of 12-18 people will be infected by that one person2.

For comparison, for each person infected with...

  • COVID-19: 2-3 people will be infected on average3
  • Influenza: 1-2 people will be infected on average4
  • Ebola: 2 people will be infected on average3
This means that measles is 6x as infectious as COVID-19!

What is herd immunity?

Herd immunity is when the population has enough immunity to a disease that the risk of disease spread is low. Herd immunity can be acquired through vaccination or through enough people getting sick with the disease and successfully fighting it off, granting them natural immunity (which is similar to what vaccines provide). The herd immunity threshold is the point where, if the percentage of people vaccinated drops below it, herd immunity weakens and the risk of a disease outbreak rises. This is why those segments are explicitly highlighted as a danger zone below.

Each disease has a different herd immunity threshold, depending on their infectivity. Measles has a very high herd immunity threshold of 95%, meaning that at least 95% of the population should be vaccinated against it to prevent an outbreak.
